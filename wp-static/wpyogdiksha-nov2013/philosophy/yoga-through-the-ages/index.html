Yoga Through The Ages

According to Indian Mythology, the tradition of yoga has been around since the beginning of Time. As the story goes, soon after the creation of the world by Brahma – the creator, Shiva who first came to the world to save Brahma from desire, and thus help the creation, become very furious with the outcome of it. He saw the never ending cycle of pleasure and pain, death and rebirth accompanied with unhappiness, misery, frustration, disease as well as viciousness and cruelty. He challenged Brahma, who pleaded: “I just created the world, not the misery. The world is neither beautiful nor ugly; neither joyful nor sorrowful; neither right nor wrong. It just is! The rest are just the perceptions of the mind. The mind can be deluded by perceptions or enlightened by the truth.”+ Whose choice is it? Who controls the mind? Shiva went to Avimukta, at Kashi, where he pondered upon these questions. He sought a way to control his mind, make it see the truth, beyond the veils of illusion. The answer lay in Yog, the means to join the individual’s mind to the way of the Universe.

Shiva sat under a banyan tree, imparting his teachings. His teachings, or a mere part of it, traveled through time by the oral tradition from teacher to student. There are few major written works on yoga. However, many aspects of Yog are discussed in great detail in The Upanishads, dating as far back as 1500BC. The Bhagvad Gita, as part of the great verse Mahabharat, speaks profusely of it.

Later, the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, considered by many as the definitive compilation of Yogic teachings in the form of 196 aphorisms (or Sūtras) came about around 2nd century BC.

More recently the Hatha Yoga Pradipika, 15th century AD is said to be the oldest surviving text on Hatha Yoga. Until very recently, yoga teaching was done on an individual basis in small ashrams. Yoga classes as we know them today have been around for less than 100 years.

The three major schools of Yoga today in the West can be traced to the same source of teaching of Sri Krishnamacharya. Three of his students founded the three schools of yoga each with a different focus and style. His student and son, Desikachar, founded the school of Viniyoga, focussing on the traditional teaching tailored to the individual student’s needs. Pattabhi Jois, founder of Ashtanga yoga, focussed on dynamic practice known as vinyasa or flow. The third school named after its founder, BKS Iyengar, reflects the need of the time by using the physical body as a practical guide to the inner world. He systematically gathered and published all the aspects of yoga practice, opening up a very wholesome approach to the subject.

One of the focal points of Iyengar yoga is the recognition of the deviation of our bodies away from nature and our inability to benefit from the traditional yogic postures alone, as the intelligence of the body is well hidden behind stiffnesses caused by generations of modern living. Many props have been introduced to make the practice accessible to students of all abilities. Precise instructions, attention to alignment and safety are trademarks of all Iyengar yoga teachers all over the world, achieved by structured and extended Teacher Training system only open to established practitioners.

Through the course of Time, the teachings of Yog have undoubtedly diversified, diluted and metamorphosed into the yoga of present day. As only Time itself is privy to the ways of time, one need not wonder whether it’s for the better or worse. It just is! The rest is a mere perception of the mind.

To seek the Truth within us, beyond the veils and endless cycles of Kāla(Time) is the beginning and end of Yog itself.
